Rubric Best Practices

Be Specific

Use clear, observable criteria. Instead of "good writing," use "correct grammar and spelling throughout."

Define Levels Clearly

Make it easy for students to understand what separates each performance level. Use distinct descriptors.

Share in Advance

Give rubrics to students before they start the assignment. This helps them understand your expectations.

Use for Feedback

Reference the rubric when providing feedback. Help students see how they performed on each criterion.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many criteria should I include in my rubric?

It depends on the assignment complexity. For shorter assignments, 3-4 criteria work well. For larger projects, 5-6 criteria allow for more detailed feedback. Start with fewer criteria and add more as needed.

What's the difference between 3-level and 4-level rubrics?

A 3-level rubric typically uses Developing, Proficient, and Advanced. A 4-level rubric adds Beginning, making it easier to identify students who need extra support. Choose 4-level for formative assessment or when you want more granular feedback.

Can I modify the rubric after generating it?

Absolutely. You can customize all criteria names and descriptions by editing directly in the table. After making changes, use the Print or Copy button to save your final rubric.

Should I use weighted scores for different criteria?

Yes, weighting can help. For example, content might be worth 50%, organization 30%, and mechanics 20%. Clearly communicate weights to students so they know what matters most for the assignment.

How often should I use rubrics in my classroom?

Use rubrics for major assignments and projects. They help standardize grading and provide consistent feedback. Even a simple rubric for smaller assignments can improve student understanding of expectations.
